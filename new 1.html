We'd Love to Own These Underrated Japanese Cars

Despite being some of the best and most unique machines to hail from the Land of the Rising Sun, no one talks about these 10 underrated cars.

By Charles Bravins Published Sep 26, 2020
Email

Japanese cars have made history in the petrolhead books. These mean machines shook the American performance car market by offering cheap and reliable power. While their low prices made people flock to the showrooms, popular films and games such as Fast & Furious and Gran Turismo made these cars even more desirable.

But those Supras, Toyota AE86s, and the Skyline GTR (R34) are the most popular of the JDM lot. What about the cars no one talks about?

There are other performance cars from great Japanese manufacturers such as Honda and Mitsubishi that have had their legacies swept under the rug. It's time to uncover them. These 10 rides are some of the best yet underrated performance machines that have been made in the East.

10 1985 Honda CRX Si

The 1985 Honda CRX Si blended power and reliability beautifully. This car was born in the first generation of the Honda CRX, which was built between 1984 and 1987. The Si, specifically, was born out of the need for more performance from the 1984 model.

The 1985 Honda CRX Si featured fuel injection technology that added more power to the base 1984 engine. What made this car tick as a great performance vehicle was its light weight. Its handling was therefore excellent, making the ’85 CRX Si a pleasant car to drive.

Even better, Honda managed to get the mileage right, which made the Si not only a car that worth racing on the road but also a pleasant one to run other errands in.

9 1975 Toyota Carina 2000GT

The Toyota Carina had a successful seven generation-run from 1970 to 2001 before the model was discontinued. And during this long tenure, Toyota tried wooing younger buyers 5 years into production, with the Toyota Carina 2000 GT.

Despite not making it to the mainstream performance cars market, the 1975 Toyota Carina 2000GT won hearts because of its impressive build quality. Under the hood, Toyota had tried to squeeze more power out of the four-cylinder engine, but that wasn’t quite enough to woo many generation Xers who enjoyed rock, pop, and a wild drive down to Las Vegas.

advertising

8 1971 Mitsubishi Galant GTO

The 1971 Mitsubishi Galant GTO was a mixture of American muscle and Japanese ingenuity. It formed part of the first generation lineup of the Galant GTO, one of the most iconic cars ever built by Mitsubishi.

Originally, the Galant GTO started life as a Colt Galant GTO, when it was first showcased at the 1969 Tokyo Motor Show. And for this first generation, Mitsubishi produced the Galant as a coupe, wagon, and sedan.

The ’71 Galant GTO took a special twist, however. Mitsubishi experimented with the then-popular American muscle car styling, featuring long hoods, rounded quad-headlamps, and rear lights, plus a raised cut-off ducktail trunk.

7 Mazda 6 MPS

Forget the iconic RX8 or the nimble Miata, the Mazda 6 MPS was a great performance car that few talked about. This can be partly blamed on the styling, which was too tame for a car whose power unit was nowhere near tamable.

This 2006 Mazda Sedan featured a mundane design. It had four doors, looked like a company car, and most drivers imagined it would drive like one, too. But that was until JDM diehards and car magazines such as AutoExpress took it out for a spin.

Under the hood of this “ordinary” Japanese build was a 2.3L power unit that produced 256bhp. The “tamed” machine could sprint from 0-60mph in 6.6 seconds.

advertising

6 Mitsubishi Galant VR-4 MK3

The Galant VR-4 valiantly carried the rich reputation of its forefathers from 18 years earlier. Produced from 1987 to 2002, the Galant VR-4 spanned across three generations of Galants, with each carrying significant improvements in performance and handling from the previous generation. And of these three, the 1996 – 2002 build was the strongest.

Known as the MK3, the styling in this car was improved, the aero kit was tuned for more performance, and the engine was beefed up to drain every bit of horsepower it could from its six cylinders. The 2.5L V6 thus produced a whopping 280hp and 268lb-ft of torque, taking the car from 0-60mph in less than 6 seconds.

5 1991 Suzuki Cappuccino

This car's size fits its name. And ironically, its performance matches the energy you would get from a full cup of this espresso.

The 1991 Suzuki Cappuccino is among the most famous Kei cars. This popularity came from the Cappuccino’s splendid mix of top performance, light weight (about 750kgs), and superb handling.

According to Suzuki, the Cappuccino boasts a 50/50 weight distribution when both seats are occupied. Couple that with the 0.66L DOHC inline-3 engine that was just at the limit of Kei cars specifications, and it's obvious why this car performed well.

advertising

4 Toyota Celica GT4

Fans of rallying talk about the great Subaru WRX STI or the Mitsubishi Evo that have dominated rallying stages ever since their release. But who mentions the Toyota Celica GT4?

This machine was a rally Viking. Produced between 1986 and 1999, the Toyota Celica GT4 was built specifically for rallying. However, at that time, regulations stipulated that a manufacturer had to produce sufficient road-going versions of the car before it could be allowed to compete.

Despite being a very successful rally car, Toyota cut the chord in 1999, and the Celica GT4 lay forgotten, buried under the later glories of Mitsubishi, Subaru, and Citroen.

3 Mitsubishi 3000GT

This was Mitsubishi’s answer to the R34. Also known as the Mitsubishi GTO back in the East, the Mitsubishi 3000GT was fitted with the best performance package Mitsubishi could get out of its factories.

Under the hood, the rampaging 3L V6 churned out an impressive 276bhp, and for some models sold in the US, the output could peak at 324hp. All this power was sent to an AWD system, either through a four-speed automatic or five-speed manual. And just like the Nissan Skyline GTR it was in mad competition with, the Mitsubishi 3000GT also featured four-wheel steering and an active aero spoiler.

advertising

2 Toyota Chaser X90 and X100

What was Toyota up to during this R34 – 3000GT feud? Messing around with the Toyota Chaser. And as far as their car was concerned, it had no competition to worry about.

This was pretty evident in the styling they chose for the Chaser X90 and X100. Instead of a track-ready coupe design, they fitted four doors to the chaser, making it inherently a sedan. But under the hood was a powerful 2.5L twin-turbo inline-6 that produced 217hp.

The only part that Toyota got right was hooking up this powerful engine to a 5-speed manual or a 4-speed automatic transmission that sent all that grunt to the rear wheels. The X90 and X100 are still popular drift cars in Japan.

1 Mazda RX7 FC3S

The RX7 is the beautiful work from Mazda that birthed the iconic Mazda RX8. And yes, the RX7 was popular. But that only applied to the encore FD RX7. Its slightly older brother, the FC RX7, did not receive much love from gearheads.

What’s bewildering is the fact that both cars are pretty much the same, only the FD happened to come out later than the FC. They were both equipped with great rotary engines, handled beautifully, and had top-of-the-line styling. Sadly, thanks to the little love the FC3S got, prices linger around $6k for a used one. Good luck getting an FD RX7 in similar condition for that price.
